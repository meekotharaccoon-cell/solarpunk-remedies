## Elderberry Syrup

**Plants:** elderberry (Sambucus nigra)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Season:** late summer to early fall (harvest), year-round (use stored syrup)

### Preparation

1. Harvest ripe elderberries in late summer (dark purple, not red).
2. Remove berries from stems using a fork.
3. Combine 1 cup dried elderberries (or 2 cups fresh) with 3 cups water in a saucepan.
4. Add 1 cinnamon stick, 4 whole cloves, and 1 tablespoon fresh grated ginger.
5. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 45 minutes until liquid reduces by half.
6. Strain through cheesecloth, pressing berries to extract all juice.
7. Let cool to lukewarm, then stir in 1 cup raw honey.
8. Store in a glass jar in the refrigerator for up to 3 months.

### Uses

- Immune support
- Cold and flu prevention
- Antioxidant boost

**Safety:** Never consume raw elderberries, bark, or leaves -- they contain cyanogenic glycosides. Always cook berries before use. Not recommended for pregnant or nursing women without consulting a healthcare provider. May interact with immunosuppressant medications.

## Chamomile Sleep Tea

**Plants:** German chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Season:** summer (harvest), year-round (dried)

### Preparation

1. Harvest chamomile flowers when petals are fully open and beginning to fold back.
2. Dry flowers on a screen in a warm, well-ventilated area for 1-2 weeks.
3. Use 1 tablespoon dried flowers (or 2 tablespoons fresh) per cup.
4. Pour boiling water over flowers in a cup or teapot.
5. Cover and steep for 5-10 minutes (longer steeping increases potency).
6. Strain and sweeten with honey if desired.
7. Drink 30-60 minutes before bedtime for best sleep support.

### Uses

- Sleep aid
- Anxiety relief
- Digestive calming
- Mild pain relief

**Safety:** Avoid if allergic to plants in the Asteraceae/daisy family. May increase effects of blood thinners and sedatives. Generally recognized as safe for most adults.

## Plantain Leaf Poultice

**Plants:** broadleaf plantain (Plantago major), narrowleaf plantain (Plantago lanceolata)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Season:** spring through fall (fresh), year-round (dried leaves reconstituted)

### Preparation

1. Identify plantain leaves in your yard (broad ribbed leaves growing in a rosette, not the banana-like fruit).
2. Harvest several fresh, clean leaves -- avoid areas treated with pesticides.
3. Wash leaves thoroughly under running water.
4. Chew or crush the leaves to release the plant juices (chewing works fastest in the field).
5. Apply the mashed leaf directly to the bug bite, sting, or minor wound.
6. Hold in place with a bandage or cloth wrap.
7. Replace with fresh poultice every 2-4 hours.
8. For a more prepared version, blend leaves with a small amount of water to form a paste.

### Uses

- Bug bites
- Bee stings
- Minor cuts
- Splinter drawing
- Rash relief

**Safety:** For external use only. Clean the area before applying. Seek medical attention for severe allergic reactions, deep wounds, or signs of infection. Ensure correct plant identification.

## Dandelion Root Tea

**Plants:** dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Season:** early spring or late fall (harvest), year-round (dried root)

### Preparation

1. Dig dandelion roots in early spring or late fall when energy is stored in the root.
2. Wash roots thoroughly, scrubbing off all soil.
3. Chop roots into small pieces (about 1/4 inch).
4. Optional: roast chopped roots at 300F for 2 hours for a coffee-like flavor.
5. Use 1-2 tablespoons of chopped root per cup of water.
6. Place roots in a saucepan with cold water and bring to a boil.
7. Reduce heat and simmer for 15-20 minutes.
8. Strain and serve. Add honey or lemon to taste.

### Uses

- Liver support
- Digestive aid
- Mild diuretic
- Nutrient-rich tonic

**Safety:** Avoid if allergic to Asteraceae family plants. May interact with lithium, antibiotics, and diuretic medications. Not recommended for people with gallbladder problems or bile duct obstruction. Harvest only from areas free of pesticides and herbicides.

## Lavender Infused Oil

**Plants:** lavender (Lavandula angustifolia)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Season:** mid-summer (harvest), year-round (stored oil)

### Preparation

1. Harvest lavender stems when about half the flower buds have opened.
2. Bundle and hang upside down to dry for 1-2 weeks in a dark, well-ventilated space.
3. Strip dried flowers from stems and lightly crush them.
4. Fill a clean glass jar halfway with dried lavender flowers.
5. Cover completely with a carrier oil (olive oil or sweet almond oil work well).
6. Seal jar and place in a sunny windowsill for 3-4 weeks, shaking daily.
7. Strain through cheesecloth into a clean jar.
8. Apply to temples for headache relief, or use as a massage oil for stress.

### Uses

- Headache relief
- Stress reduction
- Sleep improvement
- Minor burn care
- Muscle relaxation

**Safety:** For external use only. Do a patch test before first use to check for skin sensitivity. Not the same as essential oil -- this infused oil is milder and safer for direct skin application. Keep away from eyes.

## Echinacea Tincture

**Plants:** purple coneflower (Echinacea purpurea), narrow-leaf coneflower (Echinacea angustifolia)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Season:** fall (root harvest), summer (aerial parts), year-round (tincture)

### Preparation

1. Harvest echinacea roots in fall after the plant is at least 3 years old, or use fresh flowers and leaves.

6. Store in a cool, dark place for 4-6 weeks, shaking the jar daily.

### Uses

- Cold and flu support
- Immune stimulation
- Upper respiratory infection

**Safety:** Not recommended for people with autoimmune disorders. Do not take continuously for more than 8 weeks. May cause allergic reactions in people sensitive to Asteraceae family plants. Consult a healthcare provider if pregnant or taking immunosuppressants.

## Ginger Compress

**Plants:** ginger (Zingiber officinale)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Season:** year-round (ginger root available from stores)

### Preparation

1. Grate 4-5 tablespoons of fresh ginger root (do not peel if organic).
2. Wrap grated ginger in cheesecloth and tie into a bundle.
3. Bring 1 quart of water to just below boiling (do not let it boil).
4. Turn off heat and squeeze the ginger bundle into the hot water, letting it steep.
5. Dip a clean washcloth into the ginger water (use tongs -- it will be very hot).
6. Wring out excess liquid carefully.
7. Apply the warm cloth to the affected area (sore muscles, stiff joints).
8. Cover with a dry towel to retain heat. Replace when cloth cools. Repeat 3-4 times per session.

### Uses

- Muscle pain relief
- Joint stiffness
- Menstrual cramp relief
- Improving local circulation

**Safety:** Test temperature before applying to avoid burns. Do not apply to broken skin, rashes, or inflamed areas. Discontinue if skin becomes red or irritated. Not suitable for areas with poor circulation or numbness.

## Calendula Healing Salve

**Plants:** calendula (Calendula officinalis)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Season:** summer (harvest), year-round (stored salve)

### Preparation

1. Harvest calendula flowers at midday when fully open and dry.
2. Dry flowers completely on screens for 1-2 weeks (moisture causes mold).
3. Fill a jar halfway with dried calendula petals.
4. Cover with olive oil and let infuse for 4-6 weeks in a sunny spot, shaking daily.
5. Strain the infused oil through cheesecloth.
6. Gently melt 1 ounce beeswax per cup of infused oil in a double boiler.
7. Stir beeswax into the warm infused oil until fully combined.
8. Pour into small tins or jars. Let cool completely before capping. Apply to minor wounds, dry skin, or rashes.

### Uses

- Minor wound healing
- Dry skin
- Rash relief
- Diaper rash
- Chapped lips

**Safety:** For external use only. Avoid if allergic to Asteraceae family plants. Do not apply to deep or puncture wounds. Discontinue use if irritation occurs. Beeswax alternative: candelilla wax for vegan version.

## Thyme Steam Inhalation

**Season:** summer (harvest fresh), year-round (dried)

4. Place the pot on a stable surface. Sit comfortably in front of it.
5. Drape a towel over your head and the pot, creating a tent to trap steam.

8. Repeat 2-3 times daily during congestion.

### Uses

- Nasal congestion
- Sinus relief

**Safety:** Keep face at least 12 inches from the water to prevent burns. Not suitable for young children due to burn risk. Discontinue if breathing becomes difficult. People with asthma should use with caution as steam may trigger spasms.

## Comfrey Bruise Salve

**Plants:** comfrey (Symphytum officinale)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Season:** summer (harvest), year-round (stored salve)

### Preparation

1. Harvest comfrey leaves in summer when the plant is in full growth.
2. Wash and dry leaves thoroughly (any moisture will cause mold in the oil).
3. Chop dried leaves and fill a jar halfway.
4. Cover with olive oil, leaving 1 inch of headspace.
5. Infuse for 4-6 weeks in a warm spot, shaking every few days.
6. Strain through cheesecloth, squeezing out all oil.
7. In a double boiler, melt 1 ounce beeswax per cup of infused oil.
8. Stir combined mixture and pour into tins. Let cool completely. Apply to unbroken skin over bruises and sore areas.

### Uses

- Bruise healing
- Sprain support
- Sore muscles
- Bone knitting support

**Safety:** FOR EXTERNAL USE ONLY -- comfrey contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids that are toxic to the liver if ingested. Never apply to broken skin or open wounds, as compounds can be absorbed. Limit use to 10 days at a time. Not for use during pregnancy.

## Mullein Respiratory Tea

**Plants:** mullein (Verbascum thapsus)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Season:** first-year leaves best in summer, year-round (dried)

### Preparation

1. Identify mullein by its large, soft, fuzzy gray-green leaves and tall flower stalk.

4. Use 1-2 tablespoons dried leaf per cup of water.
5. Pour boiling water over the mullein leaf.
6. Cover and steep for 10-15 minutes.

### Uses

- Respiratory support
- Cough relief
- Bronchial soothing
- Lung tonic

**Safety:** Always strain thoroughly to remove fine hairs. Generally considered very safe. Mullein seeds are mildly toxic -- use only leaves and flowers. May interact with diuretic medications.

**Season:** summer (harvest fresh), year-round (dried)

## Turmeric Golden Paste

**Plants:** turmeric (Curcuma longa), black pepper (Piper nigrum)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Season:** year-round (turmeric powder readily available)

### Preparation

1. Combine 1/2 cup turmeric powder with 1 cup water in a saucepan.
2. Add 1/3 cup coconut oil or olive oil (fat helps absorption of curcumin).
3. Add 1-1/2 teaspoons freshly ground black pepper (piperine increases curcumin absorption by 2000%).
4. Stir over low heat for 7-10 minutes until a thick paste forms.
5. Let cool completely.
6. Transfer to a clean glass jar and store in the refrigerator.
7. Use 1/2 to 1 teaspoon daily mixed into warm milk (golden milk), smoothies, or food.
8. Can also be applied topically as an anti-inflammatory paste for sore joints.

### Uses

- Inflammation reduction
- Joint pain
- Antioxidant support
- Digestive aid

**Safety:** Turmeric stains everything yellow -- protect clothing and surfaces. May interact with blood thinners, diabetes medications, and stomach acid reducers. High doses may cause digestive upset. Consult a healthcare provider if you have gallbladder issues or are pregnant.

## Fresh Aloe Vera Gel

**Plants:** aloe vera (Aloe barbadensis miller)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Season:** year-round (houseplant or warm climate outdoor)

### Preparation

1. Select a thick, mature outer leaf from a healthy aloe plant (at least 3 years old).
2. Cut the leaf at the base with a clean, sharp knife.
3. Stand the leaf upright in a cup for 10-15 minutes to let the yellow latex (aloin) drain out.
4. Lay the leaf flat and slice off the spiny edges.
5. Carefully fillet the leaf by sliding a knife between the gel and the outer skin.
6. Scoop out the clear gel with a spoon.
7. Blend briefly for a smooth consistency if desired.
8. Apply directly to minor burns, sunburn, or irritated skin. Store extra gel in the refrigerator.

### Uses

- Burn care (minor)
- Sunburn relief
- Skin moisturizer
- Wound healing support
- After-shave soothing

**Safety:** For external use only unless using food-grade inner gel. The yellow latex between skin and gel is a strong laxative -- drain it fully before use. Do not apply to deep wounds or severe burns. Some people are allergic -- do a patch test first. Do not use on surgical wounds.

## Willow Bark Pain Relief Tea

**Plants:** white willow (Salix alba), crack willow (Salix fragilis)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Season:** spring (bark harvest), year-round (dried bark)

### Preparation

1. Harvest willow bark in spring when the sap is flowing and bark peels easily.
2. Select branches 2-3 inches in diameter from a healthy tree.
3. Score the bark lengthwise and peel it off in strips.
4. Scrape off the inner bark (cambium layer) -- this is the medicinal part.
5. Dry bark strips in a warm, ventilated area for 1-2 weeks.
6. Chop or grind dried bark into small pieces.
7. Simmer 1-2 teaspoons of dried bark in 1 cup of water for 10-15 minutes (decoction method).
8. Strain and drink up to 3 cups daily for pain relief. Tastes bitter -- honey helps.

### Uses

- Headache relief
- Back pain
- Mild pain management
- Fever reduction
- Anti-inflammatory

**Safety:** Contains salicin (similar to aspirin). Do NOT give to children or teenagers (Reye's syndrome risk). Avoid if allergic to aspirin. May interact with blood thinners, NSAIDs, and seizure medications. Not recommended during pregnancy or while breastfeeding. Consult a doctor if taking other medications.
