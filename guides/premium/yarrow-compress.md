## Plants & Identification

- **yarrow (Achillea millefolium)**

Always positively identify plants before harvesting. Use a reliable field guide or consult an experienced forager. When wildcrafting, harvest sustainably — never take more than 1/3 of a plant stand.

## Full Preparation Steps

**Step 1.** Identify yarrow by its feathery, fern-like leaves and flat-topped white flower clusters.

**Step 2.** Harvest aerial parts (leaves and flowers) during peak bloom.

**Step 3.** For immediate field use: crush fresh leaves and press directly onto a clean wound.

**Step 4.** For a prepared compress: steep 2 tablespoons dried yarrow in 1 cup boiling water for 15 minutes.

**Step 5.** Strain the infusion and let cool to a warm (not hot) temperature.

**Step 6.** Soak a clean cloth in the yarrow infusion.

**Step 7.** Apply to the wound and hold in place with gentle pressure.

**Step 8.** Replace compress every 30 minutes until bleeding slows.


## Uses & Applications

- Wound care
- Stopping minor bleeding
- Bruise treatment
- Nosebleeds

## Seasonal Notes

**Best time:** summer (harvest), year-round (dried)

- Harvest in the morning after dew has dried but before the midday heat.
- Flowers are best picked just as they fully open.
- Available year-round, but potency may vary by season.
- Stock up on dried material when the plant is at peak vitality.

## Safety Information

For minor wounds only -- seek medical attention for deep or serious wounds. Avoid if allergic to Asteraceae family. May increase skin sensitivity to sunlight. Not for use during pregnancy as it may stimulate uterine contractions. Do not use on infected wounds.

**General safety practices:**
- Start with small doses to check for allergic reactions.
- Keep a record of what you take and any effects.
- Do not combine multiple herbal remedies without research.
- Seek professional medical help for serious conditions.
- If symptoms worsen or persist, stop use and consult a doctor.
